Life Sustained in God: Daily Renewal Through the Spirit in the Name of Christ

A Theological Commentary

A Biblical–Theological Exposition

Introduction

The prayer “Father in heaven, in you we live, we move, and we have our being” reflects a profound biblical truth: human existence is entirely dependent upon the sustaining presence of God. Scripture consistently affirms that life itself—its origin, continuation, and purpose—flows from the divine source. Humanity does not exist autonomously but within the sustaining reality of God's providence.

This prayer also recognizes the believer's need for continual renewal through the Holy Spirit and the centrality of Christ as mediator, through whom the believer approaches the Father. Thus the prayer integrates three foundational elements of Christian theology: 

- Dependence upon the Father
- Renewal through the Spirit
- Life lived in the authority of the Son

Together these elements form a deeply Trinitarian expression of Christian devotion and spiritual life.

I. Human Existence Within the Sustaining Presence of God
Acts 17:28

“For in him we live, and move, and have our being.”

Greek Analysis

This statement appears in Paul's address at Athens and expresses a theological truth about the relationship between God and human existence. Key Greek expressions include: ζῶμεν (zōmen) — we live Derived from ζάω (zaō), meaning to possess life or vitality. This emphasizes that life itself originates from God. κινούμεθα (kinoumetha) — we move From κινέω (kineō), meaning to move, act, or function. This suggests that human activity and capacity are sustained by divine providence. ἐσμέν (esmen) — we are From εἰμί (eimi), meaning to exist or to be. This indicates that even the fundamental reality of existence depends upon God. Paul's statement therefore presents God as the sustaining ground of human existence. Life is not self-generated but continuously upheld by the Creator.

II. Addressing God as “Father in Heaven”
Matthew 6:9

“Our Father which art in heaven.”

This opening of the Lord's Prayer establishes the relational framework of Christian prayer. The term “Father” reveals both authority and intimacy.

Greek Word Study

πατήρ (patēr) — father. In biblical theology this term signifies:

- source of life
- protector and provider
- covenantal relationship.

Calling God “Father” acknowledges both dependence and belonging within the divine household. The phrase “in heaven” emphasizes God's transcendence and sovereignty while affirming that believers approach Him with reverence and trust.

III. Renewal Through the Holy Spirit
2 Corinthians 4:16

“Though our outward man perish, yet the inward man is renewed day by day.”

Greek Analysis

The word “renewed” derives from: ἀνακαινόω (anakainoō) Meaning:

- to make new again
- to restore freshness or vitality.

This renewal occurs continually through the work of the Holy Spirit, who sustains spiritual life within believers.

Titus 3:5

“He saved us, by the washing of regeneration and renewing of the Holy Ghost.” The Spirit functions as the agent of spiritual regeneration and ongoing transformation. Daily renewal therefore reflects the believer's dependence upon the Spirit's continual work.

IV. Living in the Name of Jesus Christ
Colossians 3:17

“Whatsoever ye do in word or deed, do all in the name of the Lord Jesus.”

Greek Word Study. The term “name” translates: ὄνομα (onoma). In biblical thought, a name represents:

- authority
- character
- reputation.

To live “in the name of Jesus” therefore means to live under His authority and in alignment with His character. Christ functions as the mediator between God and humanity.

1 Timothy 2:5

“For there is one God, and one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus.” Thus Christian life is lived through union with Christ, who reconciles believers to the Father.

V. The Trinitarian Structure of Christian Life

The prayer reflects a deeply Trinitarian pattern that appears throughout the New Testament. Believers:

- pray to the Father
- through the Son
- by the Holy Spirit

Ephesians 2:18

“For through him we both have access by one Spirit unto the Father.” This verse succinctly expresses the Trinitarian dynamic of Christian communion with God.

VI. The Doctrine of Divine Sustenance

The biblical worldview rejects the idea that creation functions independently from God. Instead, Scripture affirms that God continually sustains the universe.

Hebrews 1:3

“Upholding all things by the word of his power.” The Greek word φέρων (pherōn) means to carry or sustain continuously. Thus creation is not merely initiated by God but continually upheld by Him. Human life therefore unfolds within the ongoing providence of God.

VII. The Ethical Implications of Living in God

Recognizing that life exists within God's sustaining presence produces several spiritual outcomes.

1. Humility - Believers acknowledge that life itself is a gift from God.
2. Dependence - Daily renewal through the Spirit fosters reliance upon divine grace.
3. Devotion - Living in Christ's name shapes moral conduct and spiritual priorities.
4. Gratitude - Recognizing God's sustaining presence leads to continual thanksgiving.

Conclusion

The prayer “Father in heaven, in you we live, we move, and we have our being” encapsulates a rich biblical theology of dependence upon God, renewal through the Spirit, and life lived under the authority of Christ. Human existence unfolds within the sustaining presence of the Creator. The believer therefore approaches God as Father, seeks continual renewal through the Holy Spirit, and lives each day in the name of Jesus Christ.

This Trinitarian pattern reflects the heart of Christian spiritual life: a life sustained by the Father, renewed by the Spirit, and directed by the Son. As Scripture affirms:

“Of him, and through him, and to him, are all things.” — Romans 11:36

Thus the faithful life is lived in humble dependence upon God, continually renewed by His Spirit and directed toward the glory of Christ.
