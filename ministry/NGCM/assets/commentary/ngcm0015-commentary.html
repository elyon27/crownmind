The Blessedness of Meekness: Strength Under God’s Rule

A Theological Commentary on Matthew 5:5

TTU Exegetical Commentary

“Blessed are the meek, for they shall inherit the earth.”
— Gospel of Matthew 5:5

Key Doctrine

Meekness as the Posture of Those Who Belong to the Kingdom

This verse forms the third Beatitude in the Sermon on the Mount delivered by Jesus Christ. In a world that often celebrates dominance, ambition, and self-assertion, Christ declares that those who are meek are the ones who will ultimately inherit the earth. The doctrine expressed in this passage is that true strength in the kingdom of God is expressed through humble submission to God rather than through self-exalting power. Meekness therefore represents a disposition of the heart that trusts in God’s sovereignty rather than striving to secure one’s position through aggression or force.

Exposition
1. The Meaning of Blessing in the Beatitudes

“Blessed are…”

As with the other Beatitudes recorded in the Gospel of Matthew, the term “blessed” signifies more than emotional happiness. It describes a state of divine favor granted by God to those who embody the values of His kingdom. The Beatitudes reveal the paradoxical character of the kingdom of heaven: those who appear weak according to worldly standards are often those who experience the deepest blessing from God. Thus the Beatitudes redefine the nature of true success and flourishing in light of God’s purposes.

2. The Meaning of Meekness

“the meek”

Meekness is often misunderstood as weakness or passivity. In biblical theology, however, meekness refers to strength that is restrained and submitted to God’s authority. A meek person does not lack power or capability. Rather, meekness reflects the discipline of placing one’s strength under the control of God. This attitude includes humility, patience, and trust in God’s justice. Meekness stands in contrast to pride, arrogance, and the desire to assert oneself over others.

3. The Old Testament Background

The promise that the meek will inherit the earth echoes themes found throughout the Old Testament. In particular, the language reflects the promise that those who trust in the Lord will ultimately inherit the land promised by God. In this context, the inheritance represents not merely physical territory but the blessings associated with living under God’s covenant rule. Thus Christ draws upon a long-standing biblical theme: those who trust in God rather than in their own power will ultimately receive His promised inheritance.

4. The Paradox of the Kingdom

The statement that the meek will inherit the earth stands in stark contrast to the assumptions of worldly power. In many human systems, influence and possession are gained through strength, force, or political maneuvering. Yet Christ declares that the inheritance of the earth belongs to those who humbly trust in God. The paradox reveals that the kingdom of God operates according to values that differ fundamentally from those of the world.

5. The Fulfillment of the Promise

The promise that the meek will inherit the earth points forward to the ultimate restoration of creation under the reign of God. While believers may experience suffering or injustice in the present age, the final outcome of God’s redemptive plan includes the renewal of creation and the establishment of righteousness. Those who live in humble trust before God will share in this future inheritance. Thus the Beatitude offers both present encouragement and future hope.

Greek Notes

Several Greek expressions illuminate the meaning of this verse.

“μακάριοι” (makarioi)
Translated “blessed,” this word describes a condition of divine favor and spiritual flourishing.

“πραεῖς” (praeis)
The term translated “meek” refers to gentleness, humility, and controlled strength. It describes a disposition characterized by patience and submission to God.

“κληρονομήσουσιν” (klēronomēsousin)
Translated “they shall inherit,” this verb refers to receiving an inheritance that has been promised.

“τὴν γῆν” (tēn gēn)
The phrase “the earth” can refer both to the land promised to God’s people and to the broader concept of the renewed creation under God’s reign.

Together these terms emphasize that those who live in humble trust before God will receive the inheritance promised in His kingdom.

Ministry Implications

For students and ministers trained within Tenio Theological University and the mission of Narrow Gate Christian Ministry, this passage offers several important lessons.

1. Spiritual Leadership Requires Meekness
Those who serve in ministry must exercise authority with humility and gentleness rather than domination or self-promotion.

2. Strength Must Be Submitted to God
The biblical concept of meekness reminds believers that true strength lies in submitting one’s abilities and influence to God’s purposes.

3. Faith Requires Trust in God’s Justice
Believers must trust that God will ultimately establish justice rather than seeking to secure their own advantage through worldly means.

4. Future Hope Shapes Present Character
The promise of inheriting the earth encourages believers to cultivate humility and patience while awaiting the fulfillment of God’s kingdom.

Concluding Reflection

In the Sermon on the Mount recorded in the Gospel of Matthew, Jesus Christ presents a vision of the kingdom that overturns conventional expectations. The inheritance of the earth does not belong to the proud or the powerful. Instead, it belongs to those who walk in humility before God. Through meekness, believers demonstrate their trust in God’s sovereignty and their willingness to submit to His rule. And in the unfolding purposes of God, those who embrace this posture will ultimately receive the inheritance promised in the kingdom of heaven.
