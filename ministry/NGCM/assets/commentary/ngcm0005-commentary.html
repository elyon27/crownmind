The Eternal Word: The Deity and Pre-Existence of Christ

A Theological Commentary on John 1:1

TTU Exegetical Commentary

“In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God.”
— Gospel of John 1:1

Key Doctrine

The Eternal Deity of the Word (Logos)

This verse presents one of the clearest declarations in Scripture concerning the eternal nature and divine identity of Jesus Christ. The apostle John opens his Gospel with a theological prologue that establishes Christ not merely as a historical teacher or prophet but as the eternal Word who existed before creation and who shares the very nature of God.

Three foundational truths emerge from the text:
- The eternal pre-existence of the Word
- The distinct personal relationship between the Word and God
- The full divine nature of the Word

Together these statements form one of the strongest biblical foundations for the doctrine of the Trinity and the deity of Christ.

Exposition
1. The Eternal Existence of the Word

“In the beginning was the Word”

The phrase “in the beginning” intentionally echoes the opening words of the book of Book of Genesis. By using this phrase, John directs the reader’s attention back to the moment of creation itself. However, John’s statement goes even further. Instead of describing the beginning of creation, he describes the existence of the Word before creation began. The verb “was” indicates continuous existence rather than origin. This means that when the universe began, the Word was already present. Thus the Word is not part of creation but exists prior to it. This reveals that Christ is eternal, sharing in the timeless existence of God.

2. The Personal Relationship Within the Godhead

“and the Word was with God”

This statement emphasizes the personal distinction between the Word and God the Father. The phrase “with God” expresses more than simple proximity. It suggests personal relationship, communion, and face-to-face fellowship. The Word therefore exists in eternal relationship with God while remaining distinct from Him. This verse lays the groundwork for the New Testament revelation that within the one God there exists a plurality of persons. The Word is not identical to the Father, yet He shares the same divine existence.

3. The Divine Nature of the Word

“and the Word was God”

The final clause of the verse declares the full deity of the Word. John does not say that the Word was merely “godlike” or that the Word possessed divine qualities. Instead, he affirms that the Word is God in essence. This statement must be understood together with the previous clause. The Word is distinct from God the Father, yet fully divine in nature. Thus John avoids two theological errors:
- The idea that the Word is merely a created being
- The idea that the Word is identical to the Father without distinction

Instead, the verse presents a profound theological mystery: unity of essence with distinction of persons.

4. The Word as Divine Revelation

The term “Word” signifies more than speech. In biblical theology, the word of God represents God’s self-expression and revelation. By identifying Christ as the Word, John reveals that Jesus is the ultimate revelation of God to humanity. God is not merely described by Christ; He is revealed through Him. Thus the incarnation described later in the chapter (John 1:14) becomes the moment when the eternal Word enters human history.

Greek Notes

The Greek text of Gospel of John 1:1 contains several significant theological features.

“Ἐν ἀρχῇ” (En archē)
This phrase means “in the beginning,” directly recalling Genesis 1:1. It places the discussion within the context of creation while pointing beyond it to eternity.

“ἦν” (ēn)
The verb translated “was” indicates continuous existence. It contrasts with verbs that describe creation or becoming. The Word did not begin to exist; He already existed.

“λόγος” (logos)
The term “Word” carries rich theological meaning. It refers to the divine expression, wisdom, and revelation of God. In the Gospel context, the Logos is revealed as the person of Christ.

“καὶ θεὸς ἦν ὁ λόγος”
The structure of this clause emphasizes the divine nature of the Word while preserving distinction from the Father. The wording indicates that the Word possesses the very nature of God.

Ministry Implications

For students and ministers formed within Tenio Theological University and the mission of Narrow Gate Christian Ministry, this verse carries profound implications.

1. Christ Must Be Recognized as Fully Divine
The foundation of Christian faith rests on the recognition that Jesus Christ is not merely a teacher or prophet but the eternal Word who is truly God.

2. The Gospel Begins With Theology
John begins his Gospel not with a story but with a theological declaration. Ministry must therefore ground believers in deep understanding of Christ’s identity.

3. Scripture Reveals God Through Christ
All biblical interpretation ultimately leads to Christ. He is the living revelation of God’s character and truth.

4. Worship Flows From the Deity of Christ
Recognizing Christ as the eternal Word leads naturally to worship. The church honors Him not merely as Savior but as the divine Lord who existed before creation.

Concluding Reflection

The opening verse of the Gospel of John establishes the foundation for the entire Gospel.

Before creation began,
the Word already existed.

Before humanity sought God,
the Word stood in eternal communion with the Father.

And in the fullness of time, that eternal Word would enter the world as Jesus Christ, revealing God’s glory and bringing life to those who believe.

Thus John 1:1 stands as one of the most profound declarations in Scripture:
the eternal Word who walked among humanity is none other than God Himself revealed.
