Crucified with Christ: The Transforming Reality of Union with the Son of God

A Theological Commentary on Galatians 2:20

TTU Exegetical Commentary

“I have been crucified with Christ. It is no longer I who live, but Christ who lives in me. And the life I now live in the flesh I live by faith in the Son of God, who loved me and gave himself for me.”
— Epistle to the Galatians 2:20

Key Doctrine

Union with Christ as the Foundation of the Believer’s New Life

In this profound declaration, Paul the Apostle articulates one of the central theological truths of the Christian faith: the believer’s union with Jesus Christ. Through faith in Christ, believers participate in His death and resurrection, resulting in a transformed identity and a new mode of life. The doctrine emphasized in this passage is that through union with Christ the believer shares in Christ’s death to sin and receives a new life sustained by the presence and power of Christ Himself. Thus the Christian life is not merely the improvement of human behavior but the manifestation of Christ’s life within the believer.

Exposition
1. The Context of Justification by Faith

The statement recorded in Epistle to the Galatians 2 occurs within Paul’s defense of the gospel against teachings that insisted upon adherence to the Mosaic law as a requirement for salvation. Paul emphasizes that righteousness comes not through human obedience to the law but through faith in Christ. Within this argument, verse 20 illustrates the profound transformation that occurs when a person places their faith in Christ. The believer’s identity is no longer defined by legal observance but by participation in the life of Christ.

2. Participation in the Death of Christ

“I have been crucified with Christ”

This statement expresses the believer’s participation in the death of Christ. Paul does not claim that he physically experienced crucifixion but that his former life—defined by sin, self-centered ambition, and reliance upon the law—has been decisively ended through Christ’s crucifixion. Through faith, the believer identifies with Christ’s death, symbolizing the end of the old life. This participation marks the beginning of a radical transformation in which the believer’s former identity is replaced by a new existence centered upon Christ.

3. The End of the Old Self

“It is no longer I who live”

Paul’s declaration does not imply the disappearance of personal identity. Rather, it signifies that the controlling power of the old self has been broken. The former life dominated by sin and self-reliance has been replaced by a new orientation toward God. Thus the believer’s identity is no longer defined by personal achievement, cultural identity, or religious performance. Instead, it is shaped by the redemptive work of Christ.

4. The Indwelling Life of Christ

“but Christ who lives in me”

The transformation described by Paul reaches its climax in the presence of Christ within the believer. Through the work of the Spirit, Christ’s life becomes the source of spiritual vitality and moral transformation. This indwelling presence enables the believer to grow in holiness and reflect the character of Christ. Thus the Christian life is not sustained by human effort alone but by the ongoing activity of Christ within the believer.

5. The Life of Faith

“the life I now live in the flesh I live by faith in the Son of God”

Although believers continue to live in their physical bodies, the orientation of their lives has changed. The phrase “by faith” emphasizes reliance upon Christ rather than upon personal strength. Faith becomes the means through which believers remain connected to Christ and receive the resources necessary for faithful living. Thus the Christian life unfolds as a continual expression of trust in the Son of God.

6. The Motivation of Divine Love

“who loved me and gave himself for me”

The verse concludes by emphasizing the personal dimension of Christ’s redemptive sacrifice. Christ’s death is not merely a historical event but an expression of divine love directed toward individuals. The believer’s new life emerges from the recognition that Christ willingly gave Himself for the sake of humanity. This awareness becomes the motivation for faithful devotion and gratitude.

Greek Notes

Several Greek expressions illuminate the theological meaning of this passage.

“Χριστῷ συνεσταύρωμαι” (Christō synestaurōmai)
Translated “I have been crucified with Christ,” this perfect tense verb indicates a completed action with continuing results.

“ζῇ δὲ ἐν ἐμοὶ Χριστός” (zē de en emoi Christos)
Meaning “Christ lives in me,” this phrase emphasizes the indwelling presence of Christ in the believer.

“ἐν πίστει ζῶ” (en pistei zō)
Translated “I live by faith,” this expression highlights faith as the means through which the believer’s life is sustained.

“ἀγαπήσαντός με καὶ παραδόντος ἑαυτὸν” (agapēsantos me kai paradontos heauton)
Meaning “who loved me and gave Himself,” this phrase emphasizes the sacrificial love of Christ as the foundation of redemption.

Together these expressions underscore the believer’s participation in Christ’s death and life.

Ministry Implications

For students and ministers trained within Tenio Theological University and the mission of Narrow Gate Christian Ministry, this passage provides several essential insights.

1. Christian Identity Is Rooted in Union with Christ
Believers must understand their identity as participants in the life of Christ.

2. Transformation Comes Through the Indwelling Presence of Christ
Spiritual growth results from the active presence of Christ within the believer.

3. Faith Sustains the Christian Life
Continual reliance upon Christ enables believers to live faithfully amid the challenges of life.

4. Love Motivates Obedience
The recognition of Christ’s sacrificial love inspires believers to respond with devotion and gratitude.

Concluding Reflection

In the declaration recorded in the Epistle to the Galatians, Paul the Apostle reveals the profound mystery of union with Christ. Through faith, the believer participates in Christ’s death, leaving behind the old life defined by sin and self-reliance. In its place emerges a new life animated by the presence of Christ Himself. This transformed existence unfolds through continual faith in the Son of God who loved humanity and gave Himself for their redemption. Thus the believer becomes a living testimony to the transformative power of the gospel, in which the life of Christ is manifested within those who belong to Him.
